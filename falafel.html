Falafel

What is Falafel?

Falafel is a popular Middle Eastern dish made from ground chickpeas or fava beans, mixed with herbs and spices. It's usually served in a pita or on a plate, accompanied by salads, pickled vegetables, hot sauce, and tahini-based sauces.

History of Falafel

Originating in the Middle East, falafel has been enjoyed for centuries. It is believed to have been invented in Egypt, and over time, it became a staple across the region, each country adding its unique twist to the dish.

In the 20th century, falafel gained popularity among Jewish immigrants in Israel, where it became a national dish. Today, it is widely enjoyed around the world and is often considered a symbol of Middle Eastern cuisine.

Why is Falafel Popular?

Falafel is versatile and can be served in many ways. It can be eaten in a pita sandwich, as part of a salad, or as a side dish. Its rich flavor and satisfying texture make it a favorite among food lovers everywhere.

How to Make Falafel

To make falafel, start by soaking dried chickpeas overnight. Then, blend the chickpeas with onions, garlic, herbs (like parsley and cilantro), and spices (like cumin and coriander). Form the mixture into small balls or patties and deep-fry until golden brown. Serve hot with tahini sauce or in a pita with fresh vegetables.

Health Benefits

Falafel is high in fiber, which can aid digestion and promote a feeling of fullness. It also provides essential nutrients like iron, magnesium, and folate. When made with healthy oils and served with fresh vegetables, falafel can be a nutritious addition to your diet.
